The Problem

Modernisation has been broken for too long.

Bank technology leaders are asked to deliver continuous innovation on platforms designed for an era of episodic change. The two prevailing answers, replacing the core or bolting on point solutions, both fail in predictable ways. Programmes overrun. Architectures fragment. Risk accumulates faster than value is delivered.

Big-bang transformation

Multi-year core replacements promise a clean slate but routinely overrun by years and over-budget by hundreds of millions. The longer the programme runs, the further the destination drifts from the bank that commissioned it. Few finish. Fewer succeed.

Point solutions

The opposite extreme, a fintech for every gap, produces short-term wins and long-term entropy. Each integration ages independently, contracts proliferate, the canonical model fragments, and operations inherit a permanent integration tax that grows year over year.

The FinX Approach

A different model. Continuous evolution, not episodic transformation.

FinX is not a product line. It is a delivery model: a BIAN-aligned orchestration layer, an iterative engagement pattern, and a coexistence strategy that keeps the existing core productive while new capability is composed around it.

Architecture

BIAN-aligned by design

Every API, every service domain, every canonical model maps to BIAN. The architecture is not opinion. It is industry standard. Banks gain interoperability, vendor optionality, and a shared language with their ecosystem partners.

Delivery

Iterative value, every quarter

Engagements are scoped to deliver measurable business outcomes in 6 to 9 month cycles, not multi-year programmes. The bank sees value early, learns continuously, and compounds capability with each release.

Coexistence

Managed legacy, not ripped legacy

The existing core stays in place and stays productive. Glue mediates between legacy and new: exposing modern APIs over old systems while gradually migrating service domains as the business case justifies.

Innovation

Structure does not constrain innovation. It compounds it.

The standard objection to architectural standards is that they slow innovation. The opposite is true. A canonical contract gives every team a stable seam to build against. New capabilities ship without re-engineering existing ones. Each addition strengthens the platform rather than complicating it.

Speed

Structured boundaries enable speed

Clear service domain interfaces mean new capabilities can be introduced, tested, and deployed without disrupting existing operations. Time-to-market for a new product line drops from quarters to sprints.

Consistency

Consistency at scale

Standard data models and APIs ensure each new addition stays coherent with the broader platform. Innovation compounds rather than fragments. Year three of the platform is more capable than year one, not more brittle.

Ecosystem

Ecosystem integration without architectural debt

BIAN-aligned interfaces simplify integration with third-party fintechs, data providers, and regulatory systems. The innovation surface expands without increasing architectural risk. Partners plug in against a known contract.

Resilience

Competitive resilience

The ability to respond to market change rapidly, without accumulated technical debt, becomes a durable structural advantage. Banks running on composable architecture can react to regulatory change, competitor moves, or new customer expectations within a release cycle.

Architecture

Why BIAN. Why composable. Why now.

The case for BIAN-aligned, composable banking is not an aesthetic preference. It is a structural answer to a structural problem: banks need to evolve continuously without betting the firm on a single multi-year programme. This page sets out the architectural argument that anchors every other decision in FinX.

Why BIAN

Why BIAN provides the right framework.

BIAN, the Banking Industry Architecture Network, is a vendor-neutral, industry-standard reference model for banking capabilities. It provides three properties no proprietary architecture can match.

Property 01

Vendor-neutral foundation

BIAN is owned by the industry, not by a vendor. Banks that align to BIAN avoid lock-in to a single core, fintech, or integrator. Components can be swapped at the seams that BIAN defines, not at seams a vendor invented.

Property 02

Common language

Architects, vendors, and regulators converge on the same vocabulary. A "Party Lifecycle Management" service domain means the same thing in a Tier 1 bank, a fintech, and a regulator. That common language is what makes meaningful composability possible.

Property 03

Incremental change

BIAN service domains are independently addressable. A bank can modernise Payments without touching Lending, or replace a single fintech connector without rewriting consumers. Big-bang programmes become unnecessary because the architecture supports surgical change.

Service domains

Service domains FinX implements.

Each domain is a BIAN-aligned API surface in Glue, with a corresponding role-aware view in Glass.

  • Party Lifecycle Management: onboarding, lifecycle transitions, KYC outcome capture.
  • Account Management: account opening, servicing, closure, relationships.
  • Payments: initiation, clearing, exceptions across schemes (Visa, Mastercard, Fedwire, FedNow, ADX).
  • Document Directory: evidence capture, retention, retrieval across the journey.
  • Customer Case: case lifecycle, four-eyes approvals, audit trail.
  • Identity & Verification: IDV (Jumio, Thirdstream) and AML (ComplyAdvantage) orchestrations.
Coexistence

Coexistence and risk reduction.

The decisive advantage of a BIAN-aligned approach is that the bank does not have to choose between modernisation and stability. Old and new run side by side, addressed through the same canonical model.

Without a structured approach

A patchwork that never coheres.

Point integrations multiply. Each new fintech adds another bespoke seam. Channels carry orchestration logic that should live in the platform. Every release becomes a regression-test exercise across the whole estate, and every modernisation step is treated as a risk to the existing book of business.

With BIAN-aligned coexistence

Old and new addressed through one canonical model.

Legacy systems are wrapped behind BIAN service domains. New capabilities are added against the same surface. A migration is a switch behind the canonical contract. Channels, partners, and downstream systems do not need to know which side is serving the request. Risk is contained to the seam being changed.

Data & AI

Data, AI, and governance.

Composable architecture is also the precondition for trustworthy AI. The same canonical model that lets a bank swap a connector lets it govern the data feeding decisions.

Data

A canonical data spine

Events from every BIAN domain feed into the FinX Data Lake on Microsoft Fabric. Lineage, retention, and access controls are platform concerns, not project concerns.

AI

Governed AI surfaces

AI capabilities, including onboarding decisioning, alert triage, and narrative generation, sit on top of the canonical model with explicit human-in-the-loop steps and full audit. Roadmap items are clearly marked as such.

Governance

Auditability by construction

Every workflow step, every API call, every operator action carries an immutable trail. Regulators and auditors get a single view; banks get evidence that the platform is enforceable, not just configurable.

Home/Modernization/Coexistence
Path · Coexistence~9 months to first new capability

Wrap the legacy. Add the new.

Most banks do not have the option to start over. The coexistence path puts BIAN-aligned service domains in front of existing cores so new capabilities can be added without re-platforming. Channels, partners, and Glass speak only to the canonical contract; what sits behind it is an implementation detail.

Best for

Incumbent banks who can't replace the legacy core today.

Banks where the existing core runs the book of business, where regulatory and operational continuity are non-negotiable, and where a multi-year core replacement is not on the table. The coexistence path delivers new capability in months while leaving the underlying core in place.

Without vs With

Two ways to add capability to a legacy estate.

Without a structured approach

Point-to-point integration

  • Every new channel and partner integrates directly to the legacy core
  • Customer and product data fragments across systems
  • Each new capability adds to integration debt rather than reducing it
  • Compliance and audit trails are reconstructed system by system
  • Future core replacement gets harder, not easier, with every release
With BIAN-aligned coexistence

Canonical contract in front

  • Channels, Glass, and partners integrate only to the canonical BIAN contract
  • Glue mediates between the contract and the legacy core
  • New capability can ship behind the contract without touching the core
  • One canonical event stream feeds compliance, audit, and analytics
  • Future replacement of the core becomes a swap behind the seam, not a rebuild

Migration path

From coexistence to progressive.

The coexistence configuration is also the foundation of the progressive path. Once the canonical contract is in place and channels speak only to it, individual BIAN domains can be migrated off the legacy core one at a time. Each migration is contained behind the seam.

Step 01
Wrap
Glue exposes BIAN domains in front of the legacy core. Channels move to the canonical contract.
Step 02
Augment
New capability, including IDV, AML, document directory, and Glass, ships behind the contract without touching the core.
Step 03
Decouple
Domain by domain, the canonical contract becomes the source of truth. Legacy moves toward systems-of-record only.
Step 04
Replace
Optional. With the seam in place, individual domains can be migrated off legacy under the progressive path.

Path · Progressive18 to 36 month programme

Migrate one domain at a time.

A multi-year programme with continuous value delivery and reversible checkpoints. Each BIAN service domain is migrated behind the canonical contract; channels, partners, and Glass stay stable throughout. There is no big-bang cutover.

Best for

Banks ready to migrate gradually over 18 to 36 months.

Banks who already have a coexistence seam in place, or are willing to put one in as Step 1, and want to retire legacy domain by domain rather than all at once. The progressive path delivers the long-run value of full modernisation without the delivery risk of a big-bang programme.

Capability migration order

A typical sequencing.

The order below is the default UST recommendation. It moves the lowest-risk, highest-control domains first, leaving the book of business on the legacy core until late in the programme.

Wave 01
Party Lifecycle
Customer master and lifecycle moves to FinX. Reference data, no money movement.
Wave 02
Document & KYC
Document Directory, IDV, AML, sanctions. Governed onboarding end-to-end on FinX.
Wave 03
Account Management
New accounts open on FinX; legacy accounts continue in place until the agreed window.
Wave 04
Payments
Domestic and cross-border payments orchestration migrates last, once upstream domains are stable.
Decommissioning checkpoints

Legacy retires only after the seam holds.

Checkpoint 01 · Shadow

New domain runs in shadow behind the canonical contract for an agreed period. All traffic still served by legacy; FinX results compared offline.

Checkpoint 02 · Cutover

FinX becomes primary for new traffic. Legacy still authoritative for historical data. Reversible by routing change at the seam.

Checkpoint 03 · Decommission

Legacy domain is read-only, then archived. Decommissioning is gated on a clean shadow period and a successful audit cycle.

Risk gates

No wave starts until the previous wave is green.

  • Functional parity gate: canonical contract behaviour matches legacy for the in-scope domain across the agreed test corpus.
  • Operational gate: Glass operators have run the domain in production for a full SLA window with no Sev-1 incidents.
  • Compliance gate: audit trail, regulatory export, and four-eyes controls are demonstrably equivalent or stronger than legacy.
  • Reversibility gate: a documented and rehearsed routing change at the seam returns traffic to legacy within an agreed RTO.
  • Steering gate: bank, UST, and partner steering jointly sign off before the next wave begins.

Platform glossary

Defined in plain language.

Jump
BIAN
Banking Industry Architecture Network. An industry-standard reference model for the business capabilities of a bank, expressed as service domains.
Canonical model
The single, normalised representation of party, account, document, and transaction concepts that every channel and connector speaks against.
Canonical contract
The set of API endpoints and event streams Glue exposes against the canonical model. Stable across implementation changes.
Coexistence
An operating configuration in which legacy systems remain in place behind the canonical contract while new capability is added in front of them.
Composable banking
The practice of assembling banking capability from independently versioned, BIAN-aligned components rather than monolithic cores.
Connector
A configurable adapter that mediates between a BIAN service domain and a specific fintech or vendor implementation (e.g. Jumio for IDV).
Four-eyes approval
A control where a sensitive action initiated by one operator must be counter-signed by a second authorised operator before it takes effect.
FinX Glass
The operations console: the single pane of glass for CSR, compliance, supervisor, and admin roles. Built on Modyo, sits on top of Glue.
FinX Glue
The orchestration and integration layer. Exposes the canonical contract over BIAN-aligned service domains and mediates to legacy and fintech connectors behind it.
Orchestration
Coordinating a multi-step business workflow, such as onboarding, account opening, or payment, across several service domains and external connectors.
Progressive modernisation
A multi-year programme that retires legacy domain by domain behind the canonical contract, with reversible checkpoints at each wave.

A BIAN-aligned business capability (e.g. Party Lifecycle Management, Customer Position) with a defined set of control record actions.
Frequently asked questions

Common questions from evaluators and architects.

Is FinX a core banking system?
No. FinX Glue is an orchestration and integration layer, and FinX Glass is an operations console. Core banking, including ledger, accounts, and payments execution, is provided by partners such as Thought Machine Vault Core, sitting behind the canonical contract.
Why BIAN, specifically?
BIAN gives the platform a vendor-neutral foundation, a common language across the bank and its partners, and a model that supports incremental change. The white paper covers this in detail in Section 02.
Do banks have to replace their core to use FinX?
No. The coexistence path keeps the legacy core in place and puts the canonical contract in front of it. Most incumbent banks start there.
How long does a typical engagement take to first value?
Greenfield engagements typically reach first live customer in around six months. Coexistence engagements typically reach first new capability in around nine months. Progressive modernisation is an 18 to 36 month programme delivered in waves, with continuous value at each wave.
What does the canonical contract cover today?
Party Lifecycle Management, Document Directory, IDV/AML orchestration, Account Management, and Payments. The contract is BIAN-aligned and extensible.
Which fintech connectors are supported out of the box?
Jumio for IDV, ComplyAdvantage for AML and sanctions, Zoot for decisioning, Microsoft Dynamics 365 for customer engagement, Microsoft Fabric for analytics, Wolters Kluwer for regulatory reporting, Oracle NetSuite for finance, Formpipe and Thirdstream for documents and onboarding.
How is regulatory compliance handled across coexistence?
Every action against the canonical contract emits a canonical event with actor, role, rationale, and before/after state. Regulatory export reads the canonical event store. Coverage extends as more domains migrate behind the contract.
Can banks extend the canonical model?
Yes, through a controlled extensibility model documented in the Glue Developer Hub. Extensions are namespaced so they do not conflict with the canonical surface or with future platform releases.
How is data and AI handled?
Canonical events stream into Microsoft Fabric. The canonical model is the data model. AI initiatives build against governed datasets with lineage and access control inherited from Fabric.
How do I get a sandbox tenant?
Reach out via the contact details below or through your UST account team. Sandbox tenants are provisioned with synthetic data and deterministic IDV/AML connector responses for end-to-end testing.

Get started

Quickstart

Get from zero to a working Glue integration in under thirty minutes. This guide walks through tenant provisioning, authentication, your first API call, and verifying webhook delivery. By the end you will have created a party, attached a document, and seen the lifecycle event arrive in your webhook.

Before you start

  • A FinX sandbox tenant (request via your UST account team)
  • An OAuth2 client ID and secret
  • A webhook endpoint reachable from the sandbox
  • curl or your HTTP client of choice

Step 1: Authenticate

Glue uses OAuth2 client credentials. Exchange your client ID and secret for a bearer token at the sandbox token endpoint. Tokens are tenant-scoped and expire in one hour.

cURL
curl -X POST https://auth.sandbox.ustfinx.com/oauth2/token \
  -H "Content-Type: application/x-www-form-urlencoded" \
  -d "grant_type=client_credentials" \
  -d "client_id=$CLIENT_ID" \
  -d "client_secret=$CLIENT_SECRET" \
  -d "scope=glue:read glue:write"
Token scope. Request only the scopes your integration needs. Sandbox tokens are valid for 3600 seconds. Rotate them before expiry rather than waiting for a 401.

Step 2: Set tenant context

Every request requires two headers beyond Authorization: an X-Tenant header that scopes the request to your sandbox tenant, and an Idempotency-Key that makes write operations safe to retry.

Headers
Authorization: Bearer <access_token>
X-Tenant: your-sandbox-tenant-id
Idempotency-Key: <uuid-v4>
Content-Type: application/json

Step 3: Initiate a party

Call POST /v1/parties/initiate to create a new party record in the pending_kyc lifecycle state. The response includes a partyId you will use in subsequent calls. See the API Reference for the full parameter list.

cURL · Request
curl -X POST https://api.sandbox.ustfinx.com/v1/parties/initiate \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $TOKEN" \
  -H "X-Tenant: your-sandbox-tenant-id" \
  -H "Idempotency-Key: $(uuidgen)" \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{
    "type": "individual",
    "givenName": "Sample",
    "familyName": "Customer",
    "dateOfBirth": "1990-04-12",
    "residency": "GB"
  }'
Response · 201 Created
{
  "partyId": "pty_01HZ5KGQR4BVMX9W3TJNF7CAE",
  "type": "individual",
  "status": "pending_kyc",
  "createdAt": "2026-04-29T09:14:33Z",
  "tenant": "your-sandbox-tenant-id"
}

Step 4: Attach a document

Use the partyId returned in Step 3 to attach an identity document via POST /v1/documents. The Document Directory stores the reference and makes it available to IDV and AML connectors. Supported document types include passport, driving_licence, and national_id. Provide metadata and a secure reference URL; the platform stores the link, not the binary.

cURL
curl -X POST https://api.sandbox.ustfinx.com/v1/documents \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer $TOKEN" \
  -H "X-Tenant: your-sandbox-tenant-id" \
  -H "Idempotency-Key: $(uuidgen)" \
  -H "Content-Type: application/json" \
  -d '{
    "partyId": "pty_01HZ5KGQR4BVMX9W3TJNF7CAE",
    "documentType": "passport",
    "issuingCountry": "GB",
    "expiryDate": "2031-03-20",
    "referenceUrl": "https://secure-store.example.com/docs/abc123"
  }'

Step 5: Verify your webhook

As the party moves through lifecycle states, Glue emits canonical events to your registered webhook endpoint. Within seconds of the POST /v1/parties/initiate call you should receive a party.created event at your endpoint.

Webhook payload
{
  "eventId": "evt_01HZ5KHMNP2QWRX8V6FYJD93B",
  "eventType": "party.created",
  "occurredAt": "2026-04-29T09:14:33Z",
  "tenant": "your-sandbox-tenant-id",
  "data": {
    "partyId": "pty_01HZ5KGQR4BVMX9W3TJNF7CAE",
    "type": "individual",
    "status": "pending_kyc"
  }
}
Signature verification. Every webhook delivery includes an X-FinX-Signature header containing an HMAC-SHA256 digest of the raw request body, signed with your webhook secret. Always verify this signature before processing the event.

Compliance Officer

The Compliance Officer is the human checkpoint between automated KYC/AML signals and a customer's lifecycle state. They review IDV outcomes, disposition AML alerts, run sanctions screening, and apply four-eyes governance on high-risk approvals. Every action is captured against the canonical party record.

Common tasks

Review KYC results →Triage the daily inbound queue. Inspect IDV and AML side by side and disposition each case. Reviewing KYC results for a new customer

5 min readModule: Customer & Account Mgmt

Triage the daily KYC review queue, inspect IDV and AML evidence side by side, and disposition each case. This walkthrough covers the standard happy-path review for a new individual customer.

Steps

  1. Open the Task Management console. From the Glass home, select Task Management → Compliance · Pending review. The KYC review queue lists every party in pending_kyc state with completed IDV and AML evidence, sorted by SLA. Each row shows party reference, residency, risk band, and time in queue.
  2. Review the customer profile. Open a case to land on the KYC tab of the Customer & Account Management module. The IDV outcome (document type, liveness score, match confidence) and the AML screening result (PEP hits, sanctions hits, adverse media) are shown side by side, with the canonical party record and attached documents one click away.
  3. Make your decision. Choose Approve, Request more information, or Escalate to Supervisor. Approve transitions the party to active; Request more information opens a templated message to the channel; Escalate routes the case to a four-eyes queue. Every action, along with the rationale entered, is written to the audit trail.
Permission required. The Compliance Officer role is required to disposition KYC reviews. Approving a high-risk band customer additionally requires compliance.foureyes.
